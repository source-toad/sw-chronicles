<h2> The Hyperdrive Age </h2>

Roughly six to eight decades have passed since the first functional hyperdrive vessels crossed the void between stars. In that time, interstellar travel has shifted from experimental breakthrough to a fragile, developing network linking distant systems. Despite this progress, the galaxy remains largely uncharted, and movement between stars continues to demand time, precision, and restraint.

The first hyperdrives - now referred to as Mark I systems - were unstable, inefficient, and limited in range. These early drives could only manage short, carefully calculated jumps and required extensive manual navigation. At the time, hyperdrive capability was not standardized; vessels were simply considered capable of entering hyperspace or not, with designs identified by manufacturer or generation.

As the technology matured, iterative improvements led to successive drive generations, commonly referred to by their “Mark” designation. Each new mark reflected gains in efficiency, stability, and computational support. A Mark II or III drive offered modest reliability improvements over earlier systems, while later generations reduced travel time and increased safe jump distance.

Much later, a more technical classification system emerged within shipbuilding and engineering circles. Hyperdrives began to be rated by “class,” with lower numbers indicating faster and more efficient travel. This system was applied retroactively, with the earliest Mark I drives now understood to be roughly equivalent to extremely slow Class 50 systems. What will eventually be possible in under a day, now takes a month or more. These longer trips are tedious, arduous and dangerous, making them very rare occurrences, even for the most experienced traders.

In the present era, hyperdrive technology remains costly, specialized, and unevenly distributed. Most operational vessels are equipped with drives in the range of Mark II to Mark IV, capable of interstellar travel but requiring cautious, multi-stage navigation. More reliable commercial traders and organized fleets may operate Mark V or VI systems, offering improved efficiency and reduced travel times. Higher-performance drives exist but are rare, expensive, and typically restricted to well-funded organizations or government use.

Hyperdrive-capable ships themselves are not common assets. Many worlds lack the infrastructure to construct or maintain them, relying instead on visiting traders or contracted carriers.

Safe passage through hyperspace depends on precisely charted routes that avoid stars, rogue planets, gravity wells, and other hazards capable of destroying a vessel in transit. These routes are the result of decades of surveying, failed expeditions, and incremental mapping. Even along known corridors, ships rarely attempt a single uninterrupted jump, instead traveling through a sequence of shorter transitions with recalculations between each stage.

Only a limited number of reliable hyperspace corridors currently exist, linking small clusters of systems where repeated travel has confirmed relatively safe passage. Outside these routes, navigation becomes significantly more dangerous. Survey vessels tasked with expanding the network must advance in careful increments, recording hazards and transmitting data back to their sponsors. Many such expeditions are lost, and progress remains slow.

As a result, interstellar journeys remain long undertakings. Travel between nearby systems along established routes can take several weeks, while voyages that deviate from these corridors may stretch into months. Less experienced crews often default to shorter jumps or even sublight travel when uncertainty rises, prioritizing survival over speed.

The structure of interstellar trade reflects these constraints. Established hyperspace corridors act as narrow channels through which most traffic flows, creating natural choke points between regions. These routes are strategically valuable and actively contested, as vessels traveling them often cannot deviate from their plotted course.

Beyond these corridors, accessibility drops sharply. Systems that require even modest detours see far less traffic, as reaching them adds time, cost, and navigational risk. Traders rarely make such journeys unless the return justifies the effort, often consolidating cargo and commitments before departure. For many mid-rim and outlying worlds, interstellar arrivals are infrequent events - some receiving only one or two visiting vessels per standard year, with each arrival carrying months’ worth of goods, information, and external contact.

### Interstellar Communication

Communication between star systems has developed alongside hyperdrive travel but remains constrained by the same limitations of distance, infrastructure, and route availability.

Physical transport remains the most reliable method of communication. Ships traveling established routes routinely carry messages, navigational updates, and bulk data alongside their cargo.

Long-range relay transmitters have begun to appear along the most heavily traveled hyperspace corridors, allowing compressed signals to be passed incrementally between systems. While faster than courier transport, these networks are limited in both reach and capacity, and rarely extend beyond the safest and most frequently used routes.

Consequently, interstellar communication remains slow and uneven. Messages between nearby systems may take days or weeks to arrive, while communication with more isolated worlds can take months. Information spreads inconsistently, and many systems operate with only partial awareness of events beyond their immediate region.
